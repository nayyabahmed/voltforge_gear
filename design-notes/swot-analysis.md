## Internal - Strengths

- **S1 Novel combination, validated demand.** No existing product
  combines a sustained motorised artefact, engineering fundamentals,
  child-level writing and UK curriculum alignment; every neighbour
  category is healthy (see market report).
- **S2 Evidence-backed pedagogy.** Analogy-first, hands-on-verification
  method matches current research on teaching abstraction at 11.
- **S3 Authoritative blueprint.** Implements the RAEng "Thinking Like an
  Engineer" vision; Part 1 mirrors the six habits of mind.
- **S4 Quality machinery.** Research passes with traceability, safety
  conventions (SAFETY.md, adult-check), versioned review loop, glossary
  discipline. Hard for a fast follower to fake.
- **S5 Blocker-proof delivery.** Direct-to-family with sessions and kits
  - independent of school staffing and publishing categories.
- **S6 Diversified value.** Book + kits + weekly sessions monetise the
  same content three ways and create switching costs.

## Internal - Weaknesses

- **W1 Most of the book does not exist yet.** 12 of 44 chapters written;
  the cohort will consume content faster than the current authoring pace
  if unmanaged (mitigation: write ahead of cohort pace - see actions
  file).
- **W2 Solo-author bottleneck.** Research, writing, review, kits and
  sessions all depend on one person; illness or life events stall
  everything.
- **W3 Unproven retention.** No evidence yet that 11-year-olds sustain a
  44-chapter journey; first motorised build arrives two-thirds in.
- **W4 Visuals are placeholders.** Sketch placeholders mark where
  illustrations/photos belong; none produced. The book's child appeal
  depends heavily on closing this gap before print/wide release.
- **W5 Concentration.** One channel (programme), one cohort, one
  geography (UK) at launch - a single point of market failure.
- **W6 Inherited Part 1 still mid-upgrade.** Chapters 05, 06, 07, 08,
  09 and the capstone remain at v0.1 or half-reviewed; quality is
  currently uneven across the very chapters the cohort meets first.

## External - Opportunities

- **O1 Primed pipeline.** STEM Racing alumni and their parents already
  believe in car-based engineering learning; the handbook is the
  individual-depth follow-on.
- **O2 The D&T vacuum.** Parents see practical education vanishing from
  schools; a rigorous at-home alternative meets a felt loss (and the
  Bitesize tie-ins signal legitimacy).
- **O3 Falling hardware costs.** Every price drop in printers and
  electronics grows the addressable market and shrinks the Part 4 cost
  barrier.
- **O4 Community flywheel.** Cohort showcase shelves, failure stories
  and finished buggies are self-renewing marketing and curriculum
  feedback; Chapter 43 ("Design Your Own Variant") seeds a designs
  ecosystem.
- **O5 Expansion routes once proven.** Trade publisher approach with
  cohort evidence in hand; teacher notes for schools/clubs; translations;
  licensed kits. None need deciding now; all benefit from the same
  proof.

## External - Threats

- **T1 Fast follower.** A kit company or content studio could clone the
  surface (printed RC car + lessons) quickly; depth and safety rigour
  are the defence, but only if visible to buyers.
- **T2 Safety incident.** One LiPo fire or tool injury in the cohort
  would be devastating reputationally and legally. Highest-severity,
  actively mitigated threat (see actions file, Risk 4).
- **T3 Attention competition.** The real competitor is not another book
  - it is screens. Weekly sessions and keepable artefacts are the
  counter, but engagement must be earned every week.
- **T4 Supply volatility.** Kit economics depend on hobby-grade
  electronics pricing and availability (import costs, discontinued
  parts). BOM.md/COST-LEDGER discipline and modular interfaces (buy
  precision, print structure) limit exposure.
- **T5 External content drift.** Learn More boxes reference Bitesize/ETS
  pages that can move or be renamed (already seen: no KS3 "systems and
  control" page). Search-form links (house rule) rather than deep URLs
  limit breakage; research passes re-verify per chapter.

## Strategy Pairings (TOWS)

- **S-O (attack):** Use S3+S1 in parent-facing marketing aimed at O1/O2
  - "the RAEng vision, implemented at home; the depth STEM Racing
  can't fit in a term." Use O4: make cohort artefacts public early.
- **W-O (build):** Close W1/W6 by sequencing writing to cohort pace
  (Part 2 before autumn); use O4 community feedback to prioritise which
  chapters/visuals matter most; commission W4 illustrations for Part 1-2
  first, not the whole book.
- **S-T (defend):** Against T1, make S4 visible (publish sample chapters
  with research notes; the safety framework as a selling point). Against
  T2, S4's conventions are the control - never trade them for speed.
- **W-T (survive):** W2 x T2 is the worst square: a solo author cannot
  absorb a safety failure. Priority: external review of Ch 22 (LiPo)
  before the cohort reaches it, parent-facing safety onboarding, and
  insurance/legal review of programme terms before Part 3.
