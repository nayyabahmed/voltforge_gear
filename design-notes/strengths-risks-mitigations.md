# Strengths, Risks and Mitigation Actions

**Date:** 2026-07-19
**Scope:** The Young Engineer's Handbook (VoltForge Gear) - the book and
the registration programme (parts kits + weekly online sessions,
launching 27 July 2026).
**Companion:** [market-landscape-novelty-research-report.md](market-landscape-novelty-research-report.md)
- the evidence base behind the claims below.

---

## Strengths

1. **Unserved, validated demand.** Every neighbour category (RC guides,
   3D printed RC projects, kids' activity books, STEM Racing at 29,000+
   schools) is healthy; none offers depth. The gap is supply-side, not
   lack of interest.
2. **Evidence-backed pedagogy.** Modern research supports teaching
   abstraction at 11 when anchored in concrete experience - exactly the
   style guide's analogy-first, hands-on-verification method.
3. **An authoritative blueprint.** The handbook implements the Royal
   Academy of Engineering's "Thinking Like an Engineer" vision (2014)
   that the school system could not deliver; Part 1 mirrors the six
   engineering habits of mind. Strong positioning line for programme
   materials.
4. **Timing.** The enabling stack (sub-GBP 200 reliable printers, cheap
   brushless electronics, free child-usable CAD) matured ~2020-2024.
   First-mover position in a newly possible niche, with 12 chapters and
   the conventions already built.
5. **The delivery model routes around both structural blockers.**
   Schools cannot staff it -> direct-to-family with weekly sessions.
   Publishers have no category for it -> self-distribution with kits.
6. **Quality machinery already in place.** Research passes with
   traceability notes, safety conventions (SAFETY.md, adult-check line),
   versioned review loop (backlog/TRACKER), UK curriculum tie-ins.
   Expensive to build - and therefore hard for a fast follower to copy.

## Risks and Mitigation Actions

### Risk 1 - Long-middle drop-off (the biggest risk)

A 44-chapter journey demands sustained commitment; the first motorised
build (Chapter 33) sits two-thirds of the way in.

Actions:

- [ ] Treat first-cohort retention as the launch's most valuable output:
  track per-chapter completion from day one (session attendance + mini
  project photos submitted), and review at weeks 4, 8, 12.
- [ ] Front-load visible wins: every chapter's mini project produces a
  keepable artefact (already a convention - protect it in review passes);
  the showcase shelf is the retention device, treat it as such in
  sessions.
- [ ] Consider re-sequencing options if cohort data shows mid-Part 2/3
  drop-off: e.g. an early "taster" motorised mini-build (simple brushed
  motor + battery demo) pulled forward into Part 2/3 as a session
  activity, without disturbing the staged-build principles for the main
  build.
- [ ] Publish the stage map ("push at 32, drives itself at 33, you drive
  it at 34") in programme materials so families have honest expectations
  and visible milestones to aim for.

### Risk 2 - Shrinking school pipeline

D&T's collapse (~71% since 2010) means fewer children arrive primed;
discovery and motivation fall on parents.

Actions:

- [ ] Market directly to parents; lead with the RAEng habits-of-mind
  framing and the Bitesize/KS3 alignment ("supports what school no
  longer has time for").
- [ ] Position against STEM Racing as complement, not competitor: the
  at-home, individual-depth counterpart to the school team competition -
  and a pipeline for children who enjoyed it.
- [ ] Keep every chapter's no-equipment activity variant intact (style
  guide rule) so the book stays accessible to families without a printer
  until Part 4 - lowers the entry barrier.

### Risk 3 - Execution load on a solo author

The per-chapter loop (research pass, safety verification, curriculum
alignment) is what makes the book good - and slow. The moat and the
burden are the same thing.

Actions:

- [ ] Protect the review cadence: one chapter per session/branch/PR (the
  current rhythm works - keep it).
- [ ] Maintain the trackers honestly (TRACKER.md, WRITING-TRACKER.md) so
  scope is always visible; resist bumping versions before the loop is
  complete (the Ch 02/06/08 lesson).
- [ ] Sequence writing to stay ahead of the cohort's pace rather than
  finishing the whole book first: Part 2 chapters (12-19) are needed
  months before Part 4 chapters.
- [ ] Bank reusable assets as they emerge (analogy bank, mini project
  pattern, Learn More verification notes) - already happening in the
  style guide; keep consolidating there.

### Risk 4 - Safety and liability

LiPo batteries, tools and motorised builds with children: safety is the
licence to operate.

Actions:

- [ ] Keep the adult-check convention absolute (every mini project;
  extend judgement to activities like the paperclip fatigue test - done
  in Ch 04).
- [ ] Complete SAFETY.md alongside Chapter 10 (already planned) and
  cross-check every chapter's safety-adjacent claims against it in the
  research pass (already in the loop - do not skip under time pressure).
- [ ] Before the cohort reaches LiPo content (Ch 22), have the battery
  safety chapter externally sanity-checked against manufacturer and
  official guidance; kit selection should prefer safer chemistry/
  hard-case packs and include a LiPo bag.
- [ ] Add a clear parent-facing safety overview to programme onboarding
  (what supervision each part of the journey needs), derived from
  SAFETY.md.

### Risk 5 - Window closes / fast follower

The niche is newly possible and visibly attractive once demonstrated.

Actions:

- [ ] Ship the programme on schedule; cohort outcomes (photos, showcase
  shelves, retention numbers) become marketing evidence a copier cannot
  fake quickly.
- [ ] Keep the book's depth assets visible (sample chapters, the
  research-notes discipline) - they signal quality that thin copies
  cannot match.
- [ ] Revisit this document and the market landscape every 6 months or
  at each Part completion, whichever comes first.
